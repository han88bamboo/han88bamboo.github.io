    Charlie Metcalfe On The Balvenie’s Blueprint For Proper Scotch: Cask Finishing Done Right, An Enduring House Style & Its Unsung Heroes

    Copy Link Facebook WhatsApp Twitter/X Reddit Pinterest LinkedIn Email

    "We do not want to change the way that we make Balvenie to cut corners. We know it's not the most efficient, but we know it's giving us the best quality Scotch whisky that we can offer to the world."

    –Charlie Metcalfe, The Balvenie's Global Brand Ambassador

     

    In the art world, movements don’t necessarily spread overnight. But once the market learns the cue, original ideas travel fast. An artist develops a new technique to make a canvas feel alive. The breakthrough becomes a movement, and the movement soon replicates everywhere - sometimes brilliantly, sometimes merely derivative. Jackson Pollock becomes "splatter". Lucio Fontana becomes "slash a canvas". Basquiat becomes "expensive graffiti".

    The Scotch industry's famous "cask finishing" technique has followed a similar arc. It started as a breakthrough in the early 1980s, expanding whisky's expressive range through thoughtful secondary maturation. With its transformative results undeniable, the practice became broadly adopted, entering the industry's shared vocabulary. Some argue, it might even be used as a shortcut to novelty in some corners today. 

    Which is why it matters to return to The Balvenie’s pioneering role at the start of that story. Long before "cask finish” became an industry buzzphrase, former Malt Master David Stewart began exploring what could happen when mature whisky was given a final stretch in a different cask to deepen its character. That early work eventually crystallised into a bottle that has become a modern reference point: The Balvenie DoubleWood 12, whose signature is precisely a harmonious balance of two cask styles speaking.

     

     

    The Balvenie’s identity emphasises how it has kept its discipline. The distillery still champions an intentionally hands-on, decidedly uneconomic way of making whisky that carries a certain custodial pride, practising traditional floor malting, in-house coopering, and even the growing of its own barley, if only at a small scale. While it carefully preserves the core style, it has at the same time built playful, limited “chapters” through its Stories range to explore people, experiments and moments in history without asking the core style to bend out of shape.

     

    Charlie leading a Balvenie tasting in Singapore at The Distillers Library by William Grant & Sons.

     

    That sensibility is immediately apparent when you meet Charlie Metcalfe, The Balvenie’s Global Brand Ambassador. A suave, Bond-like presence in the room, he is effortlessly fluent in both the romance and the mechanics of whiskymaking, and still disarmingly warm and grounded in the Scottish way.

    In our sit-down with Charlie, the idea of balance kept resurfacing, not just in a well-finished whisky, but in how the distillery sees its role today. We spoke about how The Balvenie strives to stay true to a consistent house style through the decades, while finding resonance with new drinkers whose palates and expectations evolve. 

    To that end, we learnt how The Balvenie's popular Stories range is the distillery's release valve, to tell old and new stories without asking the core identity to contort itself. Along the way, Charlie also regaled me with tales that make the distillery feel more like a living place, from the fabled story of Balvenie Castle's Rose to other bits of Speyside mischief and lore.

    It also becomes clear to me how Charlie and his team of Ambassadors are not simply narrators of The Balvenie story, but play an active role in helping shape its profile, aided by real-world insight fed back to the Malt Masters themselves.

    What follows is a conversation about craft and continuity, yes, but also about why the people who set the standard still matter most after everyone follows the cue.

    "I really do see [cask finishing] as Balvenie and David Stewart's gift to the whisky world. I think it's an incredible thing. Before, whisky flavour was fairly set, fairly concise within these two cask types. Whereas now, it has just expanded into this incredible umbrella of flavour."

    [88 Bamboo]: The Balvenie has built out such a layered spectrum of ranges – Stories, Rare Marriages, the 50 Year Olds, the Cask Finishes – each exploring a particular facet of the distillery's heritage. What is The Balvenie’s approach to conceptualising them?

    We’d like to zoom in on the Stories range specifically. What were the initial goals of The Balvenie Team when it launched? What aspect of the reception felt memorable to you?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: Well, first of all, thank you for having me again here today. It's going to be great to have a conversation with you.

    It's really interesting thinking back to when the Stories Range was launched. I remember it like it was yesterday and it doesn't seem like all that long ago, to be honest. Really what the range set out to achieve when we launched it was – we knew, looking at our stocks, that we had some very interesting small cask experiments that were sitting maturing in our warehouses and that were coming to a very nice point in their life. And we could see the roadmap of that, if you like, for the next maybe around 10 plus years. So we were keen to bottle quite a lot of these smaller parcels of stock as either single cask whiskies or as very small vattings, and offer those as something limited and exciting for our customers around the world.

     

     

    The flip side of that was – that was one arm of it, we had these interesting small cask experiments – the second half of it was we had these incredible rare experimentations that have run through our distillery, sitting, maturing. For example, a big one is the Week of Peat. We've got this week's worth of peated Balvenie that's sitting maturing. It didn't really have a home because we bottled it originally as Peat Week, you might remember that. It was its own thing, it didn't have a home within the series. So we were keen to absorb that into a wider idea. But then we knew we had things like the Day of Dark Barley, another distillery-driven experimentation that was sitting at a great age that we wanted to bottle fairly quickly because we knew it was coming to the peak of its maturation.

    That was when we were thinking, what could we bring together in The Balvenie way that has a really unique storytelling aspect to it? We just thought, let's keep it very pure, very real storytelling. Let's talk about the people we talk about already – these incredible craftspeople that have worked our distillery for 10, 20, 30, 40 plus years. Let's speak about them, let's highlight them and let's talk about the whisky in our usual way, in the way that we like to talk about it with our Five Crafts, and really give some deep information behind the rarity of these products that we're bottling within the Stories range.

     

     

    That was the original concept and we think it's been received really well by our fans all over the world. I think it's been received super well, partly due to the fact that the whiskies are knockout whiskies. They're all really fantastic whiskies. But I think our customers have loved hearing more about the actual people as I touched on, and I think that's a side of the whisky industry that isn't always highlighted enough. People talk about Malt Masters, they're on this incredible pedestal quite rightly because they've got amazing knowledge and ability to create these whiskies. But sometimes we forget there's an incredible array of people working in the distillery behind them that don't always get the light they should.

    That was a big thing for us. It was bringing those people to the forefront and telling their stories as well. I think that's what's really resonating with this new set, if you like, that are coming into the whisky space.

    On the flip side, we have the established crew within the whisky world and they like a bit more information-led storytelling. They want to hear about the tradition, the detail behind the creation of these whiskies and I think Balvenie's perfectly set up to be able to pivot between both sets of drinkers, neither of which we shy away from because we really started with the OG set, if you like. We were very in tune with those people around the world and they're great fans of ours. In the last few years we've been enjoying pivoting towards this new set and this new approach, and certainly for me it's been very exciting.

    "We've not historically done a huge amount of wine casks, whereas the last few years we've managed to get access to stuff – heavier wines – that we feel the balance is there and you've seen that within some of the bottlings."

    [88B]: What does the reception to Stories tell you about different segments of whisky drinkers, and how The Balvenie thinks about marketing moving forward?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: It's a really interesting question actually. I think for us we've got a lot of new people coming into the Scotch whisky space and the world whisky space really that are looking for something different within their lives. They're getting these incredible immersive experiences, whether it be with fashion brands, with bars, you name it, they're getting these very immersive experiences and they want to see that from us as well.

     

     

    I think the Stories series and all of our series really are set up quite well to give these experiences. The one we saw each other at recently, the 50 Year Old launch, is an example of an incredible rare launch that we can bring in a new way of storytelling through, whether it's through audio, through taste.

     

    [88B]: The Balvenie holds on to the Five Crafts while also pioneering practices like cask finishing. The distillery grows its own barley, maintains floor maltings, and has in-house coopers and coppersmiths – which gives you independence, but presumably also flexibility to innovate.

    How does The Balvenie think about the seemingly antithetical tenets of tradition vs. innovation?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: It's a very interesting question and it's something that I've talked to David and Kelsey about multiple times. For me, the two aren't really separate. They need to coexist together for them to work properly. Because if you go too far into one or the other, you're probably going to get a little bit lost. So they have to coexist together. I think for us finding the balance is super, super important.

    The way that we want to create Balvenie is very set, but that doesn't mean that we can't bring in changes within the floor maltings, for example. I'll give you an example of a live change that's going on there at the moment. Just to help the team out a little bit, we've brought in some equipment that will help turn the floors with an electric turner. That minor piece that we brought in – we're still operating traditionally in there, but the guys aren't doing the back-breaking, hand-turning of the floors multiple times a day. That's a lot of stress on the body. This machinery just helps the team in there that little bit. They'll still use the traditional malt shiels (shovels) where appropriate and to turn floors if required. It's just that little embracing of modern technology that can help our process within that area.

     

     

    So that's a live example that literally has happened in the last few months here. I was very curious to see how the team would actually embrace this, whether they'd be like the typical Scots – "Oh, we don't need that modern nonsense." They're very open to it. At the end of the day it's not just making their life easier, it's making things operate smoother, and it's giving their body that little bit of respite. An example of innovation and tradition coming together there.

    We've certainly seen more examples of that throughout the distillery as well. We've got access to so much technology now within distilling in general, that bringing in these little things within the distillery that streamline our process and ultimately allow us to create the best quality spirit – and in years to come, Scotch whisky – we can, is something that we should be looking at and embracing.

     

    [88B]: David Stewart once said, "If you know where you came from, you have a clearer idea of where you are going."

    Where is The Balvenie going? What's in the cards for the next 5-10 years?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: I love that quote from David. I think it's a wonderful quote. I think I was there when he actually said it, and it was quite a wow moment. He summed it up really nicely.

    In terms of where the distillery is going at this point, I'll maybe touch on Kelsey, because obviously David has done so much for Balvenie over the years. It's been extraordinary. For Scotch whisky and world whisky as a whole, it's extraordinary, and you and your readers know that. One of the really exciting things going forward is what Kelsey is going to bring to Balvenie and the wider Scotch whisky industry. She's passionate, like David, about being the first to do something, the first to discover that new thing. She's got a really unwavering approach to quality and to finding that new thing.

     

    Kelsey McKechnie is the curent Malt Master at The Balvenie, who is also one of the youngest people globally to hold the prestigious title after training under legendary David Stewart. 

     

    We want to be the first to highlight this incredible new cask finish or this amazing new distilling technique. Balvenie is going to continue as it's always done to push the boundaries, but within what we feel is appropriate for Balvenie and for our spirit. We're looking at a number of things at the moment – without giving away too much – both within the cask space, so within maturation, if you like, and pushing some things within the distillery as well. And that's not going to be a massive change to cut points or the way that we've always crafted our spirit, but maybe bringing in some interesting trials, some more things that we could highlight within small parcels of stock. We'll see where those go.

    Sometimes, these experiments, if you like, or these innovations, they might not see the light of day – we might not feel they're appropriate for bottling as a single malt Balvenie. At least we tried – at least we had a look at what could be greatness.

    "Certain bourbon producers have done a Tabasco cask-finished bourbon, but [WGS] did it with Scotch and it did not work. It was a wild, wild flavour. *Laughs*"

    [88B]: Given The Balvenie is the spiritual home of cask finishing, are there new cask types the distillery is looking to explore? 

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: Cask finishing, obviously you touched on it at the start there. It's so important for The Balvenie and something that we're fiercely proud of – the fact that we brought this to the whisky world, not just Scotch whisky, but to world whisky. And I really do see that as Balvenie and David Stewart's gift to the whisky world. I think it's an incredible thing. Before, whisky flavour was fairly set, fairly concise within these two cask types. Whereas now, it has just expanded into this incredible umbrella of flavour.

     

     

    And we want to continue to push for further innovation within this space. Being the home of cask finishing, the champions of cask finishing, we want to continue to bring new things to the front. A fairly recent example of that is the Pineau des Charentes cask finish, the French Oak 16 Year Old within our core range, and a very recent one, the Montilla 12 Year Old within the Stories range. These are casks that may have been used on a very small scale within the Scotch whisky space, maybe by some independent bottlers, but they've not really been highlighted on a large scale. We're very proud that we can be one of the first distilleries to do that.

     

     

    In terms of cask finishing itself, it's a tricky thing. We've all tried whiskies that we feel are not in balance, right? You try a whisky and you're like, "Gosh, that tastes like I'm drinking a glass of sherry or a glass of wine or rum or whatever it may be." I think creating balance within cask finishing is the absolute key to creating good cask-finished whisky.

    When I've watched David before while he was training Kelsey – I sat in on some of the sessions – balance was so important because he was talking about the three things: the cask, so the wood, basically, whether that be American oak, European oak, what have you; the distillery character, so the spirit of Balvenie, those key flavours; and the cask influence. So Port, Madeira, whatever the cask finish was, all of those needed to sing from the same hymn sheet and be in balance. To do that is actually very tricky. That's where the skill of the Malt Master, he or she, comes in. That to me is the essence of cask finishing and what makes it so tricky.

    In terms of new things that we're looking at, we're looking at a few exciting casks at the moment. With the world of fine wine still resonating globally, and certainly with myself and the ambassador team travelling around the world, we see fine wine being an interest of our whisky drinkers as well. So there's a parallel there. We're looking at some wine casks at the moment that are very interesting and something that, honestly, Balvenie's not done a huge amount of. We've only done a couple of wine cask-influenced whiskies really, in our history.

    Just to give you an interesting bit of insight – David was always slightly unsure about certain wine casks with Balvenie. He thought the tannic aspect of some of the red wine casks particularly that he was getting access to didn't balance very well with Balvenie's spirit. We've not historically done a huge amount of wine casks, whereas the last few years we've managed to get access to stuff – heavier wines – that we feel the balance is there and you've seen that within some of the bottlings. Kelsey is going to start to push within this space and really explore an avenue that we haven't seen from Balvenie before when David was at the helm.

    "There's this amazing sense of being a custodian for these crafts as well, and a sense that we have to tell their story for those that have gone before and actually done this amazing work, because it's rarely seen now."

    [88B]: On a more technical perspective, it seems to us that the term “cask finishing” is used really loosely today (with most whisky drinkers thinking that it’s just a matter of chucking whisky into any another cask – at the risk of oversimplification). What is it about the art or science of cask finishing that most people don’t get, or very people really understand about?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: The balance that's needed in creating good finished whisky is really the key. But I'll just talk a little bit on the lead-up to creating a cask-finished product because that's an interesting one to delve into – it is a bit of a process.

    First of all, we need to identify a potential source for these casks, whether that be a broker or whether we purchase direct from source. That's a challenge in itself, that first point of purchase of the casks. We're lucky that we have relationships with both sets globally. We do get access to some fantastic casks and believe me, not all do. It's tricky.

     

     

    Then you need to have this vision, the idea of where you want to go with it. Normally for us, as you know, it's taking an already well-matured product, usually a minimum for us of 12 years old or up, and putting that American oak matured liquid into this finished cask for a period of time. The way that we've always liked to do it is constantly do it on a very small cask filling basis to start with. Maybe one or two handfuls of casks and sample all the way through that maturation as the finish goes on, if you like. So let's say a year. We'd pull samples very regularly, whether that be every week, two weeks, or a month, and just see how that whisky is developing.

    Within that we'd try and find what we call the sweet spot. So when I've walked you through tastings and we've tasted Balvenie together, I usually like to give – say, for DoubleWood, for example – a bracket of the cask finish. Normally for DoubleWood, it's 10 to 12 months, around that time, because it's never a set 10 months. The whisky maker, Kelsey, she will be sampling this throughout to find that sweet spot. And that's what we do within the trial. We're constantly looking for where's this bracket? Is it four to six months? Is it six to eight? Or is it closer to one year? Throughout that experimentation, we find the sweet spot and we find out ultimately whether this whisky can be a thing.

     

    [88B]: It’s fascinating to think about how you would constantly pull samples of whisky from the same cask at so many stages of its life as you finish it.

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: The way that I like to look at it is the samples are like the Malt Master's library – they're their notes. You go into these amazing sample rooms. You look at the walls, they're filled with these beautiful samples and that's the reference point for the Malt Master, that's their notes. So they can dip into that.

    If you think about it, they've got this plethora of flavour, this incredible Rolodex of flavour locked within their mind of Balvenie that goes back to, in David's case, the 1940s and 1950s, and that's what's so amazing – that they can pull on these flavour points that they've got locked in there, but also look to their notes that they have captured in glass. I think it's such an interesting thing, and that's part of the skill of the Malt Master – that retention of all of this information, whether it's from a trial for a cask finish or whether it's from flavours they've experienced within older whiskies.

    "I remember when I "became" DoubleWood 12, when I was working with Balvenie for 12 years. I've not long become Caribbean Cask 14. Then in a few years I'll become whatever – 50 Year Old – but we'll see. *Laughs*"

    [88B]: Could you tell us how The Balvenie's spirit evolves as it ages – from new make through to 30, 40 years and beyond?

    And given the distillery's library of samples going back decades, how has the 12 Year Old DoubleWood changed over the years since it was first produced?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: It's a funny one. I've been lucky to look back and taste – originally, I can't remember, we maybe didn't taste it together – The Balvenie Classic, that beautiful dumpy bottle which was the first ever cask finish. I usually have one here, but I don't know where it's gone. That Balvenie Classic being the first and then moving on into the '90s.

    In 1993, we bottled Balvenie DoubleWood as you know it for the first time. We moved from Classic to DoubleWood 12, but in essence they were very similar. They were the same cask finish at the same time in DoubleWood.

    I've been lucky enough to taste these bottlings going back all the way to then. I honestly see very little change in the spirit. The way that we create Balvenie, the scale at which we create Balvenie, has changed slightly, but the profile of the spirit is extremely similar. When I talk about the profile, I'm talking about that new make that Balvenie put into those casks all those years ago. We've looked at samples of older new makes and they still have that very sweet, cereally backbone that we want to see within our new make spirit. Honestly, I see very little change in it.

     

     

    Even if we look to the cask suppliers for our American oak casks and our European oak sherry casks – certainly the sherry cask supplier is the exact same as we used all those years ago. We have an amazing long standing relationship with our friends in Jerez. The American oak casks have been very similar. We purchase from an array of distilleries around the US, but we have a specification that we like to buy to and that's been very set. As I say, that heart, that middle cut, the spirit that goes into the cask to start its life as Balvenie is extremely similar.

    You asked me to describe how Balvenie evolves as it goes through the years. For me, I've described it as new make, that sweet, cereally note. When it gets a bit of age, up until 12 years old, that's when we start to see, at 10 to 12 years of age, we start to see these honeyed notes that Balvenie is known for start to come in. The new make sweetness is very pronounced. I don't know if you've ever sampled our new make organoleptically – within its makeup, Balvenie's new make is the sweetest within the Scotch whisky industry. A very sweet style. The sweetness, when it matures in American oak casks, usually creates this honeyed, rich, sweet style. We start to see that at 12.

     

     

    When it gets later into its teens, that's when we start to see these more intricate, light, spicy, woody notes start to come in. For me, something really changes when we get up to 21. Within PortWood, our 25 Year Old, those early 20s, we start to see this real fruity, tropical bouquet start to come into the whiskies. That's something that you normally only see with age. It's a very particular flavour.

     

     

    After that we start to see some incredible flavours come in. At 30 and 40 years old and up, you get these very unctuous, nutty notes, almost marzipan, some perfume notes coming through as well. There's some very intricate notes that we only get and we only see in these very old, very rare whiskies.

     

    [88B]: How does the distillery balance consistency with evolving palates?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: Pulling the two apart for a second, the consistency word is such an interesting one within Scotch because it can be perceived as quite a boring word. Consistency can be framed as quite boring, but if we delve into it, consistency is critical. Within Scotch whisky, Balvenie and all of our friends within the Scotch whisky space are known for producing a quality product, particularly at the younger ages that are going out to the world in larger volumes.

     

     

    So with the distillery innovating, as we've touched on earlier, consistency is important because if we start putting out a product that tastes vastly different to something that's gone out in years gone by, our core set of customers are going to be upset, and that's not something we want. Consistency is very important. For us that is where the likes of the Stories range comes in because we can offer these curiosities, these oddities, these fun experiments within that. We've got our beautiful cask finishes range for the consistent wonderful Balvenie that it is. That's how I'd answer that side of it.

    On the other hand, the new drinkers or new people that are coming into Scotch, that's where it's important for us to innovate with cask finishes as well, to offer them something new and something exciting, something interesting. I think we've been able to do that nicely. A key thing is price point as well, at nice price points throughout both ranges.     [Charlie Metcalfe]: There are so many stories, so many local stories. I'll give you some further insight to a story that we bottled within the Stories range. But because it was a very old whisky, not a lot of people got to really hear the story in full. That's the Tale of the Dog. We bottled two very old single cask whiskies – a 42 Year Old and a 46 Year Old whisky. Both highly limited.

    This links back to a bygone era when people were – let's just say – liberating whisky from distilleries. The Tale of the Dog talks about a very young Dennis McBain who was working at Balvenie at the time. He was our apprentice coppersmith – Dennis still works with us to this day. (‘Loon’ means a young man in Scotland.) And Dennis was thinking this instruction was bizarre because he’s always been told that copper is precious, and he shouldn’t mess copper tools up.     I think if you were to ask our former Malt Master, David Stewart, what one of his favourite and finest creations was, it would be PortWood 21 – one that we're particularly proud of.

    Whisky number four would probably have to be one of The Balvenie Tuns. Let's say Tun 1401, Batch One. This started this movement of creating marriages of very rare whiskies. This is an approach that we're following to this day with our 25 Year Old, 30 Year Old and 40 Year Old whiskies. That started off with the Tun – this technique of bringing together these rare old products. The Tun is very important to our story and something that the wider whisky industry globally is now using. Again, pioneered by David at Balvenie.

     

     

    The last one would be The Balvenie 60 Year Old. This is the oldest ever expression of Balvenie that we've ever bottled. A 1962 single cask of Balvenie from just after when David Stewart started at Balvenie. This was a cask he watched literally from day one all the way through to the day it was 60 years old. For me, it epitomises everything that is our old and rare selections for Balvenie. It's a good one to tell our story of bottling these wonderful rare old single cask whiskies.

    [88B]: Finally, what's the best piece of advice you've ever received?

    [Charlie Metcalfe]: That is an interesting one. It's a general bit of advice: everything within whisky revolves around patience. I was always taught by a lot of my mentors within the space that patience was critical. So whether that be when you're nosing and tasting a whisky and pulling apart those flavours, or patience within everyday life – it could be with people, it could be with aspects of your work. Whatever it was, patience was very important.

     

    Charlie, with several other fellow Brand Ambassadors from around the world charged with spreading the good word of Balvenie.

     

    It's a mantra that I've tried to always employ personally and within my work. Whether it be with your career – not constantly wanting to push, just allowing your career to develop and things to come to you – it's certainly a mantra that I employ when I taste whiskies professionally or in my free time, taking the time to give the whisky the respect it deserves. Patience is a big one.

     

    Kanpai!
